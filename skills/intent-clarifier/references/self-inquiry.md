# Guided Self-Inquiry

Use guided self-inquiry to resolve ambiguity, not to prolong the conversation. The goal is to identify the smallest amount of reflection that changes the quality of the answer.

## Five Lenses

1. **Desired change**: What should be different after this request is handled?
2. **Priority**: Which benefit matters most when not every benefit can be maximized?
3. **Boundary**: What must remain true, and what must not happen?
4. **Trade-off**: What is the user willing to exchange for speed, certainty, quality, cost, or flexibility?
5. **Evidence**: What observable result would indicate that the answer worked?

Do not force all five lenses into the response. Use only the lenses that distinguish between materially different answers.

## Selecting One Question

Ask a question only when all three conditions hold:

- the missing information cannot be inferred safely;
- different answers would produce different recommendations;
- the user can answer without doing substantial new work.

Choose the question that removes the most consequential uncertainty. Keep it neutral and single-focus.

Prefer:

- "Which matters more here: a faster first result or a solution you can maintain for a year?"
- "What is the one outcome that would make this decision worthwhile?"

Avoid:

- broad prompts such as "Tell me more";
- stacked questions containing several decisions;
- leading questions that imply the user's motive;
- asking for information that does not change the answer.

## Assumption Handling

When progress is possible without a question:

1. State the smallest material assumption.
2. Give the direct answer under that assumption.
3. Mention how the answer changes only if the alternative is important.

This keeps the interaction useful while making uncertainty visible.
